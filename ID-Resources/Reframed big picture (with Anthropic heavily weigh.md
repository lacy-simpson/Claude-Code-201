## Reframed big picture (with Anthropic heavily weighted)

Taken together, peer‑reviewed research on adult learning and human–AI interaction plus Anthropic’s AI Fluency work point to a very specific recipe: teach a **single “signature move” per surface**, let **Description skills** grow through exposure and authentic tasks, and invest your scarce training minutes in **Discernment** so adults do not confuse fluency with good judgment. This reframing is below, structured around your four original questions but with Anthropic’s model as the spine.[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5][^6]

***

## 1. What makes AI onboarding “stick” for adult learners?

### Signature moves as the core of onboarding

Anthropic’s analysis of over 50,000 Claude conversations shows that each product surface has a **signature move**—a gateway behavior that predicts nearly every other fluency indicator.[^6]

- In **Chat**, the signature move is **iteration**: users who routinely send follow‑ups to refine outputs show higher critical evaluation and better Description behaviors; “one‑and‑done” users exhibit almost no observable critical evaluation.[^6]
- In **Claude Code** and **Claude Cowork**, the signature move is **clarifying the goal**: users who clearly state what they want before execution also specify format more often, set interaction style more deliberately, and break down tasks more effectively.[^6]

This aligns with broader tech‑adoption and adult-learning findings that early, high‑leverage behaviors (habits like refining queries, specifying goals, or checking outputs) do more to shape long‑term use than front‑loaded feature tours. Onboarding that “sticks” therefore centers on *drilling the signature move* in realistic tasks rather than trying to cover everything.[^4][^5][^1]

### Practice-based, authentic, and problem-centered

Adult learning research still matters here: adults adopt tools when training connects to real, immediate problems, gives them control, and allows for hands‑on practice with feedback. Anthropic’s findings sharpen this by saying *which* practice matters most: iterating (Chat) or clarifying the goal (Code/Cowork).[^7][^1][^6]

So the “sticky” orientation looks like:

- Choose 1–2 **role‑relevant workflows** per surface (e.g., draft client emails in Chat; create data-cleaning scripts in Code; assemble a report from files in Cowork).[^5][^8]
- Design activities where every learner must *perform* the signature move several times, in their own context, with brief instructor feedback.[^1][^6]
- Treat everything else (advanced Description tools, configuration) as nice‑to‑have exposure, not the core learning outcome.[^6]

## 2. Cognitive barriers, misconceptions, and mental models

### Where Anthropic’s data meets trust and mental-model research

Anthropic’s data show that **Discernment**—how people evaluate outputs—**does not naturally improve with tenure** or feature familiarity. Users become more capable at Description behaviors over time (adding context, shaping tone) but remain inconsistent in questioning outputs, especially plausible ones. This fits with human–AI decision research documenting persistent automation bias and miscalibrated trust even among experienced users.[^2][^9][^10][^6]

Other well-documented cognitive issues remain:

- **Search-engine mental model**: Adults often treat AI assistants like chatty search engines, expecting retrieval rather than generation and underestimating hallucination risk.[^11][^12]
- **Overtrust and undertrust**: Some users over‑rely on AI recommendations, others ignore them, and both patterns persist unless trust is calibrated via experience and explicit instruction.[^3][^10][^2]
- **Anthropomorphization**: Education research indicates that human-like framing (“the AI understands…”) encourages users to treat systems as intentional agents, which distorts expectations and blame.[^13]


### Addressing these in orientation

Anthropic’s recommendation is explicit: **if training time is limited, Discernment is where to concentrate it**. That dovetails with research showing trust calibration improves when users are asked to predict errors, critique outputs, and use “cognitive forcing” strategies. In practice:[^10][^2][^6]

- Pair every signature-move exercise with a **“now question it” step**: highlight that iteration or goal clarity creates *more* opportunity for discernment, not less.[^2][^6]
- Use language that **de‑anthropomorphizes** the tool (system, model, generated output) and explicitly explain that observing diffs or clean prose is not sufficient; plausible-but-false content is a known risk.[^13][^6]
- Make **Discernment behaviors explicit skills**: verifying facts, challenging assumptions, checking for missing context—taught as part of the workflow, not as generic warnings.[^14][^2][^6]

## 3. First-use experiences and habits that predict sustainable use

### Signature moves + Description spectrum as keystone habits

Anthropic’s “AI Fluency Index” shows that some skills **do** tend to emerge with continued exposure (Description behaviors), while others don’t (Discernment). Specifically:[^6]

- As users spend more time with Claude, they more frequently add examples, specify tone, and set interaction style—moving along the **Description spectrum** from in-the-moment shaping (extra context, files) toward durable configuration (Projects, CLAUDE.md, workflows).[^6]
- These Description skills **cluster with tenure and exposure**; people “find their way” to them, especially if the interface surfaces them.[^6]

This resonates with TAM/UTAUT findings: **performance expectancy, effort expectancy, and facilitating conditions** (including good defaults and configuration tools) drive continued use. Early experiences that teach users *there is a spectrum* of ways to shape the system and show a few concrete options nudge them into that organic growth trajectory.[^4][^5]

### Habits that matter early

Combining Anthropic with broader research suggests three keystone habits:

1. **Always iterate (Chat) or clarify the goal (Code/Cowork)**
    - Users who behave this way become more fluent and better at self-directed exploration.[^1][^6]
2. **Use at least one Description lever beyond the bare minimum**
    - For example, “include audience + tone” in every work prompt, or attach a real file rather than pasting fragments, to connect AI to real workflows.[^12][^6]
3. **Perform a Discernment check before accepting outputs**
    - A small, repeatable checklist (e.g., verify critical facts, check alignment with policy, inspect for missing constraints) strengthens oversight without overwhelming.[^14][^2][^6]

In short: first-use experience should feel like **rehearsal of three micro‑habits**—signature move, at least one Description upgrade, and a Discernment check—inside a real task context.[^5][^1][^6]

## 4. Structuring 40–60 minute live sessions for behavior change

### Anthropic’s 3-step curriculum as the backbone

Anthropic proposes a simple curriculum model, validated against their behavioral fluency indicators:[^6]

1. **Teach the signature move first**
2. **Advance along the Description spectrum**
3. **Revisit Discernment at every step**

Adult learning and workplace training research fit neatly around this. Adults benefit from activating prior knowledge, deliberate practice with feedback, and explicit transfer planning. A 50‑minute session for a mixed audience therefore aims to:[^15][^7][^5][^1]

- **Anchor in authentic tasks** relevant to their roles.[^5][^1]
- **Drill the surface-appropriate signature move** in those tasks.[^6]
- **Expose a few Description options** that they can continue exploring.[^12][^6]
- **Make Discernment visible and practiced** through structured critique and checklists.[^2][^6]
- **End with an implementation intention** that ties these behaviors to recurring work cues.[^4][^5]

This design uses Anthropic’s internal data as the spine, with broader peer‑reviewed work supplying the learning and organizational behavior scaffolding.
