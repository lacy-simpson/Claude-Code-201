## Big picture: what research says

Across adult learning, workplace tech adoption, and human–AI interaction research, four themes repeat: orientation must be problem-centered and practice-based, trust must be actively calibrated, early use must be scaffolded into habits, and short live sessions only “stick” when they are embedded in workflow and followed by guided practice and social reinforcement. Below, each of your four questions is addressed with peer‑reviewed or scholarly sources and then distilled into design moves you can implement.[^1][^2][^3][^4]

***

## 1. What makes AI onboarding “stick” for adult learners?

### Adult learning and AI-specific onboarding

- Adult learners adopt new tools when training is anchored in immediate, authentic tasks, gives them agency, and includes time to struggle productively with real examples.[^5][^1]
- AI-focused training with health professions students showed that understanding “how to ask better questions,” applying AI to discipline-specific scenarios, and then reflecting on correctness and limitations led to deeper, retained changes in how learners used AI later.[^1]
- Research on adult learners using AI tools for self-directed learning suggests that repeated, purposeful prompting in their own context builds ongoing use, while one-off exposure without integration into real tasks tends to decay quickly.[^6][^7]


### What differentiates lasting behavior from short-lived curiosity

Across workplace tech adoption (TAM / UTAUT) and AI-in-learning work, lasting behavior change is associated with:

- **Clear performance expectancy**: Adults must see a concrete improvement in task performance (time saved, quality improved) during training, not just hear about it.[^8][^3]
- **Low perceived effort (effort expectancy)**: Initial workflows must feel simple and not add friction; early complexity reduces intention to use and long-term adoption.[^3]
- **Social and managerial reinforcement**: When managers model use and expectations are explicit, employees keep using AI; when it remains optional and invisible, usage declines.[^4]
- **Facilitating conditions and scaffolds**: Checklists, prompt templates, and “guardrails” that are easy to retrieve in the flow of work support continued use.[^9][^7]

For onboarding design, this implies a session where learners:

- Work on real tasks from their role with AI,
- See tangible benefit within the session,
- Leave with simple artifacts (prompt patterns, decision checklists),
- Know when and how their manager expects them to apply this tomorrow.

## 2. Cognitive barriers, misconceptions, and mental models of AI assistants

### Common mental models and misconceptions

Research on lay mental models and anthropomorphization shows several recurring patterns:

- **AI as search engine**: Many users see conversational AI as “Google but chatty,” leading to shallow querying and misunderstanding of generative, non-indexed outputs.[^10][^7]
- **Overtrust / automation bias**: Human–AI decision research documents “automation bias,” where users overweight AI recommendations, under-check errors, and allow skills to atrophy, especially when the AI seems highly competent.[^11][^12]
- **Undertrust and disuse**: Other users discount AI outputs even when they are accurate, particularly when tasks are identity-relevant or when they have prior negative experiences with automation.[^2][^3]
- **Anthropomorphization**: Education research on AI anthropomorphization finds that human-like language (“understands,” “thinks”) and persona design encourage learners to treat AI as an intentional agent rather than a statistical model, with downstream effects on trust and blame.[^13]


### Evidence-based ways to address these in training

- **De‑anthropomorphizing language**: Guidance for educators recommends describing AI as “systems” that “process” and “generate” based on data and parameters, and explicitly avoiding language that implies human-like understanding or agency.[^13]
- **Trust calibration activities**: Work on trust calibration and explainable AI shows that interactive tasks in which learners must predict when AI will be wrong, compare multiple AI outputs, and rate their own confidence before/after seeing the AI can improve alignment between trust and actual reliability.[^12][^2]
- **Metacognitive prompts**: Introducing cognitive forcing strategies (e.g., “List two ways this output could be wrong”) can help learners resist automation bias and maintain critical oversight.[^14][^12]

For your orientation session, this suggests explicitly surfacing mental models (“How are you currently thinking about AI?”), then using guided comparison tasks and language discipline to reshape those models.

## 3. First-use experience and habits that predict sustainable AI adoption

### Predictors of continued, effective use (TAM/UTAUT + adult learning)

The technology acceptance literature converges on a small set of predictors of ongoing use: performance expectancy, effort expectancy, social influence, facilitating conditions, hedonic motivation, perceived value, and early habit formation.[^3]

AI and adult learning research points to similar early behaviors that predict sustained use:

- **Frequent, low-stakes experimentation**: Adult learners who “play” with AI in diverse, self-chosen contexts are more likely to keep using and refining their prompting behavior.[^7][^6]
- **Iterative prompting and reflection**: Learners who engage in multi-step prompting (refining prompts, critiquing outputs, and asking “why”) develop more sophisticated strategies and maintain use longer than those who accept first outputs.[^2][^1]
- **Embedding in existing workflows**: Workplace survey data indicate that when AI is integrated into standard processes and supported by policy, usage becomes habitual; when tools remain “extra,” adoption lags.[^8][^4]


### Design implications for first-use

For a first-use experience, the research supports:

- Starting with **one or two canonical workflows** (e.g., “draft client emails,” “summarize meeting notes”) and guiding learners through prompt–critique–refine cycles so they feel both benefit and control.[^7][^1]
- Providing **simple, visible scaffolds** (prompt recipes, “responsible use” and oversight checklists) that are tied explicitly to these workflows.[^9][^7]
- Ending with **implementation intentions** (“Next week, use this AI checklist when you draft your weekly report”) to link specific cues and contexts to future AI use, which behavior change research shows improves follow-through.[^4][^3]

## 4. Structuring 40–60 minute live sessions for behavior change

### What works for short, mixed-skill, adult sessions

Adult learning and workplace training research emphasize:

- **Activation of prior knowledge**: Briefly eliciting existing beliefs and experiences at the start increases engagement and helps correct misconceptions.[^15][^1]
- **Deliberate practice with feedback**: Time is best spent on guided, hands-on tasks with immediate feedback rather than extended lecturing.[^5][^1]
- **Concrete, near-term transfer**: Adults are more likely to change behavior when they plan how to apply the new skill within their own work, supported by specific tools and social expectations.[^8][^4]
- **Reflection and debrief**: Structured reflection consolidates learning and supports metacognitive skill, particularly when dealing with AI’s limitations and risks.[^12][^1]

Given a diverse audience, using role-based scenarios and small-group work allows learners to anchor AI behaviors in their own domain while still benefiting from common underlying principles.[^16]

## Step-by-step design for a 50‑minute AI orientation (with exemplars and pitfalls)

Below is one concrete structure aimed at behavior change, not just awareness. Each step assumes a mixed-skills workplace audience and a general-purpose AI assistant (e.g., Copilot/ChatGPT/Gemini-like).

### 0. Pre-session design choices (before the hour)

Design moves:

- Identify 2–3 **core workflows** per major role (e.g., operations: draft SOP updates; sales: email outreach; managers: summarize reports).[^4]
- Create 3–5 **prompt recipes** and one **responsible use checklist** tailored to those workflows (e.g., “Avoid sensitive data; verify load-bearing facts; adapt tone to policy”).[^9][^7]
- Coordinate with managers to set **post-session expectations** (e.g., “use AI for first drafts of weekly update emails” over the next two weeks).[^4]

Common design error: running a generic “AI overview” without linking to specific tasks, leaving learners unsure when to use AI and leading to weak transfer.[^3][^8]

***

### 1. Opening: goals, norms, and mental models (0–8 minutes)

Objective: Activate prior knowledge, surface mental models, and set a calibration frame.

Flow:

1. **Set expectations (2–3 minutes)**
    - Frame session goal as “Use AI safely and effectively for [top workflows],” not “learn about AI generally.”[^1][^5]
    - State norms: experimentation, skepticism, and no requirement to be “techy.”
2. **Quick poll / prompt storm (5 minutes)**
    - Ask: “Right now, how do you think of AI at work: search engine, assistant, co-pilot, or something else?” Have them vote or share in chat.[^10][^13]
    - Invite 2–3 comments about positive and negative experiences.

Exemplar: A Miro/whiteboard with columns “Search engine,” “Assistant,” “Black box magic,” where participants drop digital sticky notes describing their current view.

Common errors:

- Starting with long conceptual lectures on AI history or model architectures; this often exceeds cognitive load and feels irrelevant.[^5]
- Using anthropomorphic metaphors (“Our AI is like a new colleague”) that reinforce unhelpful mental models.[^13]

***

### 2. Demo + paired critique: show value and limits (8–20 minutes)

Objective: Demonstrate clear value in a realistic task while normalizing skepticism and oversight.

Flow:

1. **Live demo of a canonical workflow (6–7 minutes)**
    - Example: “Draft a first-pass response to a customer email” using a pre-anonymized scenario.[^6][^7]
    - Narrate design decisions: include task, role, audience, constraints, and ask for a draft plus 2 alternatives.
2. **Group critique (6–7 minutes)**
    - Ask participants in pairs/small groups to annotate the AI output: “What’s good?”, “What’s risky or wrong?”, “What would you change?”[^12][^1]
    - Debrief: highlight both quality and failure modes (hallucination, style mismatch, missing policy constraints).
3. **Micro-lesson on oversight (a few minutes)**
    - Introduce your **AI oversight checklist** (e.g., based on risk-awareness training: verify key facts, avoid sensitive data, check audience fit).[^9][^12]

Exemplar:

- Slide with “Before sending AI‑assisted work, always:” and 4–5 items: verify names/numbers, remove sensitive data, adapt tone, ensure policy compliance, log high-risk edits.[^9]

Common errors:

- Only demoing “wow” use cases without showing any failures, which fosters overtrust.[^2][^12]
- Failing to tie oversight behaviors to specific steps in the workflow (e.g., “always do this before copying content out of the AI interface”).

***

### 3. Hands-on, role-based practice (20–40 minutes)

Objective: Build first-use behavior with real tasks, reflection, and peer learning.

Flow:

1. **Role-specific breakout tasks (12–15 minutes)**
    - Participants select one of 2–3 tracks (e.g., “Communication,” “Analysis/Summarization,” “Planning”).[^16][^7]
    - In each track, they:
        - Take a real or near-real work artifact (email, report, task list).
        - Use a **prompt recipe** to generate a first draft, then refine it at least once based on their own critique.[^7][^1]
        - Apply the **oversight checklist** before declaring the output “usable.”[^9]
2. **Mini debrief (5–7 minutes)**
    - Ask each group to share: one win (time saved, clarity) and one limitation or surprise.[^6][^1]

Exemplar prompt recipe for a communication track:

- “You are helping me draft [type of message] to [audience] about [topic]. Use [tone]. Constraints: [policy or compliance notes]. Draft a concise version and list 3 bullet points explaining why you chose this structure.”[^1][^7]

Common errors:

- Having participants practice with artificial toy tasks (e.g., “write a poem about our company”) that do not translate to real workflows.[^5]
- Allowing “one and done” prompting rather than requiring at least one iteration and explicit critique, which research suggests is key for developing skillful AI use.[^2][^1]

***

### 4. Habit formation and implementation intentions (40–47 minutes)

Objective: Link learning to concrete future behavior in context.

Flow:

1. **Identify one target workflow**
    - Ask each learner to name one recurring task where AI can help (e.g., weekly report, meeting agenda).[^4]
2. **Implementation intention exercise**
    - Have them write: “When I [trigger], and I need to [task], I will use [AI tool] with [this prompt recipe and checklist].”[^3][^4]
    - Encourage sharing with a colleague or manager where appropriate.
3. **Discuss constraints**
    - Ask: “When should you **not** use AI for this task?” Link to risk levels, data sensitivity, and policy.[^17][^9]

Common errors:

- Ending with “Any questions?” instead of concrete if–then planning, which reduces the likelihood of behavior change.[^3]
- Ignoring organizational policies, leading to either unsafe use or complete avoidance.[^17][^9]

***

### 5. Reflection, Q\&A, and follow-through (47–50 minutes)

Objective: Consolidate mental models and signal ongoing support.

Flow:

1. **Reflective question (2–3 minutes)**
    - Prompt: “What is one way your view of AI has changed today, and one safeguard you plan to use going forward?”[^12][^1]
2. **Outline post-session supports (remaining time)**
    - Share where to find: prompt recipes, oversight checklist, policy summaries, and a channel or office hours for AI questions.[^4][^9]
    - Briefly restate manager expectations for using or piloting AI in key workflows.[^4]

Common errors:

- Treating the session as a one-off event without any visible support or follow-up, which is associated with low retention and weak habit formation in workplace training.[^8][^4]

***

## TL;DR of the step-by-step

- **Make it concrete**: Design the session around 2–3 real workflows and show AI improving those tasks live, with immediate participant practice.[^1][^5][^4]
- **Calibrate trust**: Include at least one activity where AI is obviously flawed and learners must critique and correct it using a simple oversight checklist.[^12][^2][^9]
- **Support first-use habits**: Have participants perform a full prompt–critique–refine cycle on their own work artifact and articulate a specific if–then plan for using AI in one recurring task.[^7][^3][^4]
- **Rewire mental models**: Explicitly surface and discuss how they currently think about AI, use system-based language (not anthropomorphic), and tie AI to “assistant” rather than “oracle.”[^10][^13]
- **Embed in the ecosystem**: Align with policies and manager expectations, provide simple reusable scaffolds (prompt recipes + checklist), and make ongoing support visible so the session is the beginning of a practice, not the end.[^8][^9][^4]
